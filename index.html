I. Introduction

In as much as the sole purpose for the existence of an electrical distribution system lies in supplying the requirements of the consumer's utilization devices, it is fundamental that a knowledge of these load requirements is necessary for sound planning of the distribution system and its various components. The economic importance of the knowledge of load characteristics has become more and more recognized with the growth of load on utility systems.

In recent years, much work has been done in this field. Many utilities have made and are continuing to make, in some form, surveys of the load on their systems. Information obtained from utility load surveys can be used for many purposes other than distribution system planning and optimum equipment design. Often, data from the same load survey is used in studies of rate structure, cost analysis, and development of selective selling programs.

II. Nature of Loads

  • The load that an individual customer or a group of customers presents to the distribution system is constantly changing.
  • Every time a light bulb or an electrical appliance is switched on or off, the load seen by the distribution feeder changes.

III. Loads - Definition of Terms

Loads - Electrical power needed in kVA or kW.

  • To indicate a device or a collection of equipment which uses electrical energy.
  • The power required from a given supply circuit.
  • The electrical load that indicates the current or power passing through the line or machine.

Notes from the Module

  1. All these loads will have peak demands at different times and for different durations.
  2. Industrial and commercial loads may have two peak load periods.
  3. Agricultural loads are seasonal and vary very differently.
  4. Lighting loads such as street lighting may have almost zero demand during daytime and nearly constant load during nighttime.

Load Curves and Load Duration Curves

Load Curve

Represents load in its time sequence. It is obtained by plotting station load along the Y-axis and the time when it occurs along the X-axis. Usually, the plot is for one day using average load based on a time interval Δt (commonly one hour). The area under the curve represents the total energy consumed by the load in one day.

Load Duration Curve

Represents the same load-versus-time data, but ordinates are rearranged in magnitude sequence (not time sequence). Greatest load is plotted on the left, then lower loads toward the right, and least load on the extreme right.

  • The areas under the load curve and load duration curve are equal.
  • Each area represents total units consumed during the period (for daily case: 24 hours).
  • Load duration curves can be daily, weekly, monthly, or annual.
  • Sometimes plots are done in per-unit values.

Load Characteristic Terms and Formulas

The load characteristics play an important role in:

  • Determining total power and energy requirements of the system.
  • Planning the installed capacity of a power plant.
  • Selecting suitable generating capacity for each unit of the plant.

Demand

Load at receiving terminal averaged over a specific period of time; power requirement (in kVA, kW, or A) at the load averaged over a specified voltage level.

Demand Intervals

Time interval specified for demand (Di), usually 15 minutes or 30 minutes. Obtained from daily demand curves or load duration curves.

Maximum Demand (Peak Load)

The greatest of all demands that have occurred during a specified period (daily, monthly, yearly). It may always be less than connected load.

Connected Load

Sum of continuous ratings of all load-consuming apparatus connected to the system.

Average Load

Average power consumed by the load in a given period (usually day or month):

Average Load = WT = kWhrNo. of Hrs

A.L. DAILY = kWhr generated in a day24 Hrs

A.L. MONTHLY = kWhr generated in a monthNo. of Hrs in a month

A.L. YEARLY = kWhr generated in a year8760 Hrs

W = electrical energy consumption in period of hours (T)

T = periodic time in hours

Demand Factor

Demand Factor = Maximum DemandTotal Connected Load

It is always less than 1.

Load Factor

Load Factor = Average LoadMaximum Demand

Indicates how well the utility facilities are being utilized.

Diversified Demand and Maximum Diversified Demand

  • Diversified Demand: demands imposed by a group of loads over a period.
  • Maximum Diversified Demand: sum of demands imposed by a group of loads over a period.

Diversity Factor

Diversity Factor = Sum of individual maximum demandsMaximum system demand

Utilization Factor

Utilization Factor = Maximum DemandRated System Capacity

Indicates how well electrical device capacity is being utilized.

Loss Factor

Loss Factor = Average Power LossPower Loss at Peak Load

Referred to variable power losses (copper losses, conductor/winding losses), not no-load losses in transformers.

Under steady load: Loss Factor = Load Factor

For short-lasting peak: Loss Factor = (Load Factor)²

= 3300 W6000 W = 0.55 = 55%

The lower the demand factor, the less system capacity required to serve the connected load.

Demand Factor = 30 MW50 MW = 0.6 = 60%
